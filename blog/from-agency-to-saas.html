From Agency to SaaS: What I Learned Running Both

January 22, 2025 · 8 min read
Entrepreneurship SaaS Strategy

After running a marketing agency for years, pivoting to product-led growth required rewiring everything I thought I knew about building a business.

The agency was good. We were profitable, growing, and genuinely helping clients. But somewhere around year three, I noticed a ceiling. Revenue was tied to headcount. Every new client meant new hiring. The math only worked up to a point.

So I started building software. Here's what that transition actually looked like — and what I wish I'd known going in.

The Agency Founder's Mental Model Is Wrong for SaaS

In an agency, you get paid for outcomes. A client hires you to grow their revenue by 30%, you do it, they renew. The feedback loop is tight. You know what's working. You can adjust quickly.

SaaS broke every instinct I had developed over four years.

The feedback loops are longer. A product decision you make today might not show up in churn six months from now. Marketing that feels successful — traffic, demos, signups — can be completely disconnected from the metric that matters: whether customers stick around and expand.

The first thing I had to unlearn was the bias toward activity. Agencies optimize for visible work. Meetings, deliverables, reports, check-ins. SaaS rewards invisible leverage — the architecture decision that makes scaling easier, the onboarding flow that reduces churn by 8%, the pricing change that unlocks a new segment.

What Actually Transfers

Not everything was wrong. Some agency skills translated better than I expected:

  • Client communication. Years of translating complex work into plain language made me a better storyteller for investors and prospects.
  • Commercial instincts. I could read a room, understand what a buyer cared about, and adapt a pitch on the fly. This is underrated in early-stage SaaS.
  • Speed. Agency work trains you to ship imperfect things under pressure. This is actually the right default when you're pre-product-market fit.
  • Network leverage. My agency clients became early SaaS customers. The trust was already there.

The Cash Flow Gap Nobody Warns You About

The dirtiest secret of the agency-to-SaaS transition: you will have significantly less money for longer than you think.

Agency revenue is relatively predictable. Monthly retainers, project fees, occasional upsells. SaaS revenue starts small, grows slowly, and eats cash in ways that feel invisible — AWS bills, contractor fees, tool subscriptions — before it starts compounding.

I kept the agency running for 18 months while building the product. This was the right decision. It was also exhausting and slowed product velocity. There's no clean answer. But if I were doing it again, I would have been more deliberate about setting a hard transition date instead of letting the agency drag on indefinitely as a safety net.

The One Thing I'd Tell Agency Founders

Build something you can sell to the clients you already have. The hardest part of early SaaS is trust — getting someone to put a credit card in and trust that your product will do what it says. Your existing clients have already extended that trust.

Your agency is not a distraction. It's a distribution channel. Use it.

The rest — pricing, product, positioning — you'll figure out as you go. The one thing you can't manufacture is a warm audience that already believes in you.
