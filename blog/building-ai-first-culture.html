Building an AI-First Culture at a 12-Person Startup

December 18, 2024 · 7 min read
AI Leadership Culture

AI-first isn't a tech decision. It's a hiring, process, and mindset shift that has to start at the top.

When I tell investors or prospects we're an AI-first company, I know what they're imagining: LLMs, vector databases, neural networks in every pipeline. That's part of it. But the more important version of AI-first isn't the tech stack. It's the default behavior of every person on the team.

What "AI-First" Actually Means Day-to-Day

In practice, AI-first means this: before anyone does a manual task more than twice, they ask whether AI should be doing it instead. Before anyone ships a workflow, they ask whether there's an AI-augmented version that delivers the same outcome in less time with fewer errors.

This sounds obvious. It's not. Most people — smart, capable people — have strong intuitions about how work gets done, and those intuitions were formed in a pre-AI environment. Unlearning them takes deliberate effort and explicit modeling from leadership.

The Hiring Signal That Matters Most

We started filtering for what I think of as "AI comfort" early in our hiring process. Not AI expertise — we have engineers for that. Comfort. The ability to pick up a new AI tool, experiment with it without hand-holding, form an opinion about its quality, and integrate it into their workflow.

The fastest proxy: ask candidates to show you how they currently use AI in their work. Not "do you use AI?" but "show me." The ones who have a real answer — who pull up their actual prompts, their actual workflows, who can speak to what works and what doesn't — are the ones who will thrive in an AI-first environment.

The ones who give a vague, aspirational answer about "leveraging AI" are telling you something different.

Building Shared AI Practices

We run a weekly session called "AI Show and Tell." Fifteen minutes. One person demonstrates something AI-related they've built or discovered. It doesn't have to be technically impressive. It just has to be useful.

The effect on culture has been significant. It creates a permission structure for experimentation. It normalizes sharing half-finished ideas. And it means the whole team builds on each other's discoveries instead of each person reinventing the wheel in isolation.

The other practice that's made a difference: we document our AI failures as rigorously as our successes. When an AI-powered workflow breaks, ships incorrect output, or turns out to be slower than the manual alternative, we write a short post-mortem and share it with the team. This keeps the culture honest and prevents the kind of AI cheerleading that can lead to real production errors.

The Leadership Obligation

None of this works if leadership isn't visibly doing it.

I write my first draft of almost everything with AI assistance now. I do my research with AI tools. I prototype solutions using AI before I bring them to engineering. And I talk about all of this openly, including the times it doesn't work or produces garbage.

If you want an AI-first culture, you have to model AI-first behavior. The team is watching to see whether "AI-first" is a strategy slide or a lived reality. Make it the latter.
